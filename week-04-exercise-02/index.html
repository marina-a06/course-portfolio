Frank Lloyd Wright Homes I Have Visited

This is my collection of photographs of homes I have visited that are designed by Frank Lloyd Wright. Each home was a personal experience I had with the home during my visit. This collection showcases my travels and the beautiful designs of Frank Lloyd Wright.

Frank Lloyd Wright Home and Studio

FLW | 1889-1909 | Oak Park, Illinois

This home was Frank Lloyd Wright's personal home and studio from 1889 to 1909. This home today is preserved as a museum that is open to the public. Wright expanded this home over the years as his family grew. Originally the home was designed in a Queen Anne style, while the additions to the home and studio became a Prairie Style design, that originated by Wright.

Frank W. Thomas House

FLW | 1901 | Oak Park, Illinois

Located in Oak Park, Illinois, Frank Lloyd Wright designed this home for Frank Thomas in 1901. This home is a tradition style Prairie Style home. The rooms of the home are elevated and unique to the area, this home did not have a basement. This home has the features of Wrights designs including a low hanging roof, built in shelves and cabinets and casement windows. This home is beautiful in its design of solid concrete elements connecting with nature.

Laura Gale House

FLW | 1909 | Oak Park, Illinois

The Gale House was created during one of Wright’s most productive times working in the Prairie style. Experts in architecture see it as an important step in the early growth of modern architecture. Wright’s use of simple geometric forms in both the design details and overall structure may have influenced the modern European architects of the 1920s. Among his Oak Park, Illinois projects, the Gale House stands out as one of his most distinctive designs. It has a compact layout made of interlocking rectangular shapes built around a central fireplace. The strong rectangular forms on the outside are unlike anything else Wright designed before or after this house.

Hills-Decaro House

FLW | 1906 | Oak Park, Illinois

Located at 313 Forest Avenue in Oak Park, Illinois, the Hills DeCaro House is a significant remodeling by Frank Lloyd Wright from 1906, transforming an 1883 Stick style Victorian home into one of his early mature Prairie style works. Wright's intervention rotated the house 90 degrees on its lot, applied a stucco and dark wood banded exterior, deep overhanging eaves, and horizontally grouped windows; all hallmarks of his Prairie style. Internally, the remodel introduced open flow spaces, built in furnishings and a strong connection between the indoors and outdoor terraces. After a devastating fire in 1976, the house was carefully reconstructed based on Wright's original drawings under the stewardship of later owners, the DeCaros, hence the combined name. Today the Hills DeCaro House stands as a landmark of early 20th century American architecture, illustrating Wright's transition from his experimental work into the full expression of the Prairie School

Moore-Dugal Residence

FLW | 1985 | 1923 | Oak Park, Illinois

Completed in 1895, this is Frank Lloyd Wright's first independent commision in Oak Park after Wright left the office of Adler and Sullivan in 1893. On December 23, 1922 a fire destroyed the third and fourth floors of the home. Wright immediately returned to Oak Park in 1923 to redesign and rebuild the home for a second time.

Frederick Robie House

FLW | 1910 | Chicago, Illinois

The Robie House, designed by Frank Lloyd Wright and completed in 1910 in Chicago's Hyde Park neighborhood, stands as the quintessential example of the Prairie style of architecture. Its low profile, horizontally stretched massing, long bands of art glass windows, and deep overhanging eaves all draw inspiration from the flat Midwestern landscape. Inside, the house abandons the traditional compartmentalized rooms in favor of an open plan centered around a massive fireplace that separates living and dining spaces, allowing light to flood in through the ribbon windows. Built of Roman brick with limestone bands and supported by steel framing, the home also tucked its entrance away in a recessed court, enhancing the sense of discovery and flow. This pioneering design was a key moment in American residential architecture, signaling a shift toward modernism and harmonizing interior space, furniture, and landscape into a unified whole.
